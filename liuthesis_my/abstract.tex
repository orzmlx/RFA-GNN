Drug development is a long, expensive, and high risk process, and a large proportion of candidate drugs fail before reaching the market. One of the major challenges is the complexity of biological systems, where drugs interact with genes, proteins, and signaling pathways in highly interconnected networks. These complex interactions make it difficult to accurately predict the molecular effects of drugs and to understand how drug perturbations propagate through cellular systems.

Recent advances in biological data collection have produced large knowledge bases that describe molecular relationships, such as gene–gene, protein–protein, and signaling interactions. In addition, several databases provide information about drug–gene interactions and the genes whose expression is affected by specific drugs. Integrating these heterogeneous data sources offers an opportunity to improve computational models for predicting drug responses.

In this thesis, we propose a model that combines large scale biological knowledge networks with drug target information in order to predict drug effects on gene expression. By leveraging prior knowledge of molecular interactions together with known drug targets, the model can better capture how perturbations propagate through biological networks. Such predictions can support drug development by helping identify potential mechanisms of action, prioritizing promising drug candidates, and reducing the cost and time associated with experimental screening.

We evaluate performance under unseen drug and unseen cell splits and compare against baselines including a multilayer perceptron that does not use the gene interaction graph. Under a single dose and single time point setting, the proposed graph model improves unseen cell performance by 24.4\% relative to the multilayer perceptron baseline. In addition, attention weights in graph attention layers provide an interpretable view of which gene interactions are emphasized during prediction.
