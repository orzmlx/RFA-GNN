\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background}
In 2025, AstraZeneca disclosed a failure case known as “compound 12”, which was once considered a promising lead candidate but failed before entering human trials. In animal studies, the compound caused severe muscle damage and wasting. Subsequent investigations suggested that although the drug successfully engaged its intended target, the KRAS protein, it also exhibited off target interactions that led to unexpected toxic effects~\cite{DrugHunter_2025}. This example illustrates why predicting drug effects is challenging. Hitting the intended target does not necessarily mean that a drug will be safe, because unintended interactions and downstream network perturbations can still produce harmful biological responses.

Drug discovery is challenging. Even top research organizations often fail in drug development, and only about 12\% of drugs that enter clinical trials are ultimately approved~\cite{cbo2021rnd}. One major reason is the complexity of biology. A compound may affect its intended target, but the downstream cellular response can still be difficult to predict because signals spread through interconnected networks of genes and proteins and depend on the cellular context~\cite{Brown_2025, AnnualReviews_2025}, This uncertainty involves two linked challenges, selecting a plausible target and predicting the molecular response after perturbation. This thesis focuses on the second challenge.

\section{Drug perturbation}
To specify this prediction problem, we first describe what drug perturbation means. Drug perturbation refers to the process how cells respond to an intervention. When a compound enters a cell, it works by binding to one or more proteins. This interaction can change the function of these proteins, either by activating or inhibiting its activity.

This first interaction sets off a chain reaction. Inside the cell, the shape of this targeted protein will be altered and  physically touch or stick to a downstream protein, When it makes contact, those downstream proteins also change in turn. One change leads to another and In this way, a message travels from one protein to another, spreading the original effect far beyond the drug’s first target. Eventually, some of these messages reach the nucleus because all genetic instructions are stored there and special proteins called transcription factors will be influenced, they decide which genes are turned on or off. As a consequence, the levels of gene expression are changed.

Gene expression tells us how actively each gene provides instructions for making proteins and its expression level reflects how many copies of these instructions are being produced. These expression changes can be quantified using high-throughput experiment.

\section{High-throughput experiment}

To model drug effects, we need a data source that links controlled drug perturbation to measure gene expression. The key idea is to compare treated and untreated conditions under similar experimental settings, so that observed differences mainly reflect biological responses to the perturbation rather than technical variation. This requires a standardized experiment with consistent protocol, broad compound coverage, rich metadata for matching controls and process hundreds of samples, genes, or compounds simultaneously in a single run, we call this type of experimental method as high-throughput experiments.

In this thesis, our analysis is based on the LINCS L1000 dataset. It is a typical high-throughput experiment that measures gene expression responses for many compounds across different cell lines. Experiments are performed in multipe well plates, where each well contains cells treated with a compound or a control treatment. At a fixed time point after treatment, expression is measured for 978 landmark genes, and expression for additional genes can be inferred computationally.

In each plate, two types of conditions are included:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Treatment wells}:  These receive a specific drug dissolved in DMSO. Each well gets a different drug or a different dose, allowing researchers to test thousands of compounds at once.
    \item \textbf{Control wells}: These receive only DMSO~\cite{Santos_2003}, the same solvent used to dissolve the drugs, but without any drug. These controls are essential because they show what normal gene activity looks like when no drug is present. 
\end{enumerate}

For each treatment profile, we calculate its gene expression response by comparing it to a control sample tested under similar experimental conditions. In addition to expression values, LINCS provides metadata including cell line, dose, time, and batch or plate identifiers. These details are important because experimental conditions can affect the measured results. In our preprocessing pipeline, controls are selected to match treatments by cell line and plate, this help reduce shared technical effects and make the reponse more comparable across experiments.

\section{Prior knowledge of molecular interactions}
As mentioned, drug response is effected by networks of molecular interactions, if our model predict genes expression without this relationships, it may miss important biological context. so we hope there is a large knowledge database that collecting molecular relationships from different resources and providing structured and integrated information about regulatory and signalling interactions, so that we could use it as prior knowledge graph in our model. OmniPath is one example. It combines interactions from many different sources such as pathway databases, signaling maps and provides directed relationships, sign annotations for stimulation or inhibition when available, It also includes metadata, such as how many databases or publications back up a relationship of interaction. In this thesis, we build a directed interaction network from OmniPath, and map its gene identifiers to match those used in LNCS L10000 dataset.



\section{Aim}
This thesis addresses the following modelling problem. Given a baseline cellular expression profile, a description of a drug, and a gene interaction graph, can we predict the gene expression change after treatment? This is challenging because the mapping from target engagement to expression change is indirect, depends on cell context, and is affected by incomplete knowledge of mechanisms.

Two types of external information can improve prediction. First, knowledge bases such as OmniPath provide curated gene and protein interactions. Second, compound annotations provide drug targets and chemical fingerprints. This thesis studies how to combine these sources in a graph based model.
The aims of this thesis are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item To develop a graph neural network model built on the OmniPath gene interaction graph that predicts drug induced expression changes by integrating expression inputs and drug descriptors.
    \item To evaluate generalization with unseen drug and unseen cell splits, where test drugs or cell lines are not present during training.
    \item To compare against baselines that do not use a gene interaction graph, and to use ablation tests to quantify the contribution of drug inputs.
\end{enumerate}

Among graph neural network variants, graph attention networks (GATs)~\cite{velickovic2018graph} are well suited to noisy biological graphs because they learn weights for neighbour aggregation. These weights can also be inspected as a form of model interpretation, which helps link predictions to candidate regulatory relationships.

\section{Related works}
Graph neural networks are now widely used in bioinformatics because many biological systems are naturally described by graphs. A recent review summarizes GNN approaches for single cell omics data and surveys common tasks and model variants~\cite{li2025gnn_sc_review}. These models build on general GNN ideas and architectures such as GCNs, GraphSAGE, and GATs~\cite{scarselli2009gnn, kipf2017semi, hamilton2017inductive, velickovic2018graph}.

Drug perturbation prediction is supported by large scale resources that measure gene expression under controlled conditions. The LINCS L1000 and Connectivity Map efforts provide gene expression profiles across many compounds and cell lines~\cite{l1000_paper}. These datasets enable supervised learning of drug induced expression changes. At the same time, they highlight key challenges, including context dependence, measurement noise, and limited coverage of cell models.

Several approaches use supervised learning without explicit interaction graphs. DeepCOP~\cite{woo2020deepcop} uses a multilayer perceptron with drug descriptors to predict gene regulation effects. This design is simple and scalable, and it serves as a strong baseline. It treats genes as independent outputs and it does not use known molecular connections among genes and proteins.

Another line of work focuses on drug representation and drug sensitivity prediction. tCNNS~\cite{liu2019tcnns} encodes drugs from SMILES strings and uses cell line features to predict responses. GraphDRP~\cite{nguyen2020graphdrp} represents drugs as molecular graphs and predicts IC50 values~\cite{ic50_review_2015}. These models target drug sensitivity endpoints and they emphasize chemical structure. They do not model a gene interaction graph that links drug targets to downstream transcriptional changes.

Knowledge based and graph informed methods aim to capture how perturbation effects propagate through molecular networks. Regulation Flow Analysis uses curated interaction data to infer candidate mechanisms from large knowledge resources~\cite{roca2025rfa}. Graph structured models have also been proposed for drug response tasks that use pathway or regulatory structure~\cite{wang2022graphgrp, kim2021gcn_drug_response}. These studies support the idea that molecular interactions can act as a useful inductive bias when the label signal is noisy or indirect.

Graph based learning has been used for perturbation prediction in single cell settings. GEARS uses a gene relationship graph to predict responses to genetic perturbations in single cell data~\cite{roohani2023gears}. Cellograph models multi condition single cell datasets with graph based learning~\cite{shahir2024cellograph}. Recent work also explores graph structured neural networks for perturbation biology more broadly~\cite{evans2024gsnn}. This literature motivates using graph neural networks to connect perturbations to downstream molecular effects through structured interactions.

The graph itself can represent different biological entities and relations. Single cell studies often use cell cell graphs, cell gene graphs, or gene gene graphs, depending on the task and the data type~\cite{li2025gnn_sc_review}. In this thesis, the graph represents molecular interactions and nodes correspond to genes that are aligned to the expression measurement. The modelling assumption is that network connectivity provides a useful approximation to potential information flow from targets to downstream genes.

Interpretation is another motivation for using graph based models. In many drug discovery settings it is not enough to predict an outcome. It is also useful to suggest plausible routes that connect a perturbation to a response. Explainable graph models aim to highlight relevant substructures and provide human readable summaries of model behaviour~\cite{wang2025xgdp}. Attention in GATs provides a practical way to inspect which edges are weighted more strongly during neighbour aggregation.

Evaluation practices also vary across related work. Many benchmarks focus on performance in random splits. In perturbation prediction, it is important to test generalization to unseen drugs and new cellular contexts. Software frameworks and benchmarks for drug response prediction provide useful comparisons across models and datasets~\cite{jiang2024deepdr}.

This thesis studies drug induced transcriptional response prediction with a curated interaction graph. The model integrates baseline expression, drug targets, chemical fingerprints, and OmniPath interactions~\cite{turei2016omnipath}. Generalization is evaluated with unseen drug and unseen cell splits. Attention weights are analyzed as an interpretation signal, with the goal of producing pathway level summaries that are consistent with prior knowledge.

\section{Ethical considerations}
This thesis uses public datasets from LINCS L1000 and OmniPath. The data do not include personal patient identifiers. The work does not involve human subjects or clinical interventions. The main ethical issues are related to interpretation, generalization, and the use of model outputs.

The model predicts gene expression changes after drug treatment. These predictions should be treated as computational hypotheses. They can help prioritize compounds and guide follow up experiments. They should not be used to guide clinical decisions without careful validation. A major risk is over interpretation of model outputs as evidence of efficacy or safety.

The model also produces attention based summaries that highlight edges used during message passing on the interaction graph. This can support interpretation and hypothesis generation. Attention weights are not causal proof. They reflect how the model pass information under the constraints. A pathway found by attention can be consistent with known biology, and it can also arise from graph topology, data bias, or noise in the measurements.

This study inherits limitations from both the experimental data and the prior knowledge sources. LINCS profiles are measured in a limited set of cell lines, and many cell lines are cancer derived. Results may not transfer to primary cells or tissues. Experimental settings such as dose, time, and plate can influence measurements. Matched controls and delta targets reduce some shared technical variation, and residual effects can remain.

OmniPath comes from published papers, but number of datas in different areas are imbalanced. Well studied pathways and popular genes can have more reported interactions. Less studied mechanisms can be under represented. This can bias both learning and interpretation. Predictions can be less reliable for drugs, cell types, or pathways that have weak coverage in curated resources.

We can also make mistakes if we strongly trust the model’s rankings and choices. A model can be used to rank compounds or targets for follow up work. It can also be used in a way that hides uncertainty and ignores failure cases. Reporting both average performance and difficult cases across drugs and cell lines helps reduce this risk. Simple uncertainty estimates can further support responsible interpretation, especially when the model is applied outside the training distribution.

We also need to consider environmental and resource impacts. Training deep models requires compute and storage, and the total cost increases with repeated experiments and hyperparameter search. The models in this thesis are trained on standard hardware with a limited set of configurations. This supports a reasonable balance between experimental exploration and resource use.

Reproducibility is important for ethical and scientific reasons. The code and data processing steps are documented to support transparent comparison. Random splits and model training can introduce variability. Reporting the evaluation protocol and key hyperparameters helps reduce ambiguity and supports fair comparison with future work.
